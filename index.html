Train speed without losing the plot

Fifteen minutes a day. Speed goes up only when comprehension stays up — that gate is the whole method.

What the research actually says. Typical adults read 200–400 words per minute. Controlled studies (Rayner et al., 2016) find comprehension drops sharply past ~500–600 wpm — the bottleneck is language processing, not eye speed. Realistic gains from training are 20–50%, mostly from cutting habitual re-reading, widening your fixation span, and reading more. That's what this app trains. Anyone promising 1,000+ wpm with full comprehension is selling skimming.

The daily session — 15 minutes

  1. 3min
    Warm-up · Pacer drill

    Run the pacer at your comfort speed, chunk size 2. Follow the highlight, don't let your eyes jump back. This suppresses regressions before you push speed.

  2. 8min
    RSVP sprints · 3 rounds

    Round 1: comfort +25%. Round 2 (overspeed): comfort +50% — comprehension will wobble, that's the point. Round 3: comfort +10%, which should now feel slow. Take the quiz when a round uses a built-in passage.

  3. 4min
    Speed test · every other day

    Read a fresh passage at natural (not forced) speed, then take the quiz. This measured wpm + comprehension pair is your real progress — the drills are just practice.

The progression rule

Your comfort speed starts at your baseline test wpm. After each test: comprehension ≥ 75% twice in a row → raise comfort speed 10%. Comprehension < 60% → drop it 10% and hold. Never chase the overspeed number — it's a training stimulus, not your reading speed.

Ground rules

Use easy material for drills — vocabulary you already know. During tests, read everything once; no skimming, no peeking back. And log every session: the Progress tab only ever counts sessions with a comprehension score, so the numbers stay honest.

Speed test

Read at your natural speed, then answer four questions. You'll get a measured wpm and a comprehension score — both are logged.

Passage

The timer starts when the passage appears and stops when you press “I've finished”. Passages you've already tested are marked.

RSVP sprints

Words flash at a fixed point — your eyes never move, so the pace is fully controlled. The red letter marks the optimal recognition point; keep your gaze locked on it.
